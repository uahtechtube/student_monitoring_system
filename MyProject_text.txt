CHAPTER ONE 
INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Background of the Study 
Higher education institutions globally generate enormous volumes of student academic data in 
every academic cycle. These data include results of continuous assessments, semester 
examinations, attendance records, assignment scores  and cumulative grade point averages 
(CGPAs). Despite the richness of information embedded in these records, much of it is stored and 
referenced only for administrative purposes such as grading, transcript generation  and statutory 
reporting to regulatory bodies (Romero & Ventura, 2010). The potential of this data to support 
early identification of students struggling academically  or to reveal systemic patterns in student 
performance, remains largely untapped in many developing country universities. 
The scale of student enrolment in Nigerian universities underscores the urgency of this challenge. 
According to the National Universities Commission (NUC), Nigeria had over 170 accredited 
universities as of 2022, with a total student enrolment exceeding 1.9 million across undergraduate 
programmes (NUC, 2022). Managing the academic progress of such a large student population 
through manual review of records is inherently impractical and susceptible to error. Lecturers and 
academic advisors may be responsible for hundreds of student s simultaneously, making it nearly 
impossible to detect early warning signs of academic decline through unaided observation alone. 
Academic monitoring which involves the systematic tracking  and analysis of student academic 
progress over time, is a critical institutional responsibility. Effective academic monitoring enables 
universities to identify students at risk of academic failure, implement targeted interventions  and 
improve graduation rates. Traditional academic monitoring in Nigerian universities is l argely 
reactive intervention occurs only after a student has failed a cours e or been placed on probation, 
rather than being proactive  and predictive (Adeyemi & Adeyemi, 2014). This reactive posture 
results in missed opportunities to support students before their situations become irreversible. 
The emergence of data science  and machine learning has opened new frontiers in educational 
research and institutional management. Machine learning refers to a branch of artificial 
intelligence that enables systems to learn patterns from data and make inferences or classifications 
without being explicitly programmed for each scenario (Mitchell, 1997; Bishop, 2006). In the 

=== PAGE 3 ===
 
 
domain of education, machine learning techniques have been applied to problems ranging from 
dropout prediction and course recommendation to performance classification  and early warning 
system design (Baker & Yacef, 2009; Kotsiantis et al., 2010). 
One of the most widely studied machine learning techniques in educational data analysis is 
clustering. Clust ering is an unsupervised learning technique that identifies natural groupings 
within a dataset without relying on pre labelled categories (Han, Kamber & Pei, 2012). Among the 
various clustering algorithms available, K means clustering is one of the most commonly used due 
to its computational efficiency, conceptual simplicity and scalability to large datasets (Jain, 2010). 
K means operates by iteratively assigning data points to a predefined number of clusters (K) based 
on minimizing the d istance between each data point and  its nearest cluster centroid (MacQueen, 
1967; Lloyd, 1982). 
When applied to student academic data, K  means clustering can partition the student population 
into meaningful performance groups , for example, high  performing students, moderatel y 
performing students  and academically at  risk students. These clusters can provide actionable 
intelligence for university administrators, enabling them to design differentiated academic support 
programs, allocate resources more effectively and monitor trends in academic performance across 
student cohorts (Dutt, Aghabozrgi & Maizatul, 2015). 
Educational Data Mining (EDM) is the interdisciplinary field dedicated to developing  and 
applying analytical methods for exploring data originating from educational environments (Baker 
& Yacef, 2009). EDM encompasses a wide range of techniques including classification, 
regression, clustering, association rule mining and sequential pattern analysis. The application of 
EDM has demonstrated measurable improvements in academ ic outcomes in several international 
universities that have integrated data driven monitoring systems into their administration (Romero 
& Ventura, 2010; Pena,Ayala, 2014). 
In the Nigerian higher education context, however, the integration of machine learni ng and data 
mining techniques in academic monitoring systems remains limited. Most universities, including 
Kashim Ibrahim University, KIU, currently rely on conventional manual processes for academic 
record keeping and student performance review. While som e institutions have adopted student 
information systems (SIS) for data storage, these systems typically serve as passive repositories 
rather than active analytical platforms. The lack of intelligent analytics capabilities means that 

=== PAGE 4 ===
 
 
large volumes of valuable academic data go unanalyzed and critical intervention opportunities are 
frequently missed. 
This study therefore proposes the design and implementation of a machine learning based student 
segmentation and academic monitoring system as a case study of Kashim Ibrahim University, KIU. 
The system will apply K means clustering to student academic data including CGPA, attendance 
rate and assessment scores to segment students into performance groups and provide a dashboard 
based interface for administrators and academic staff to monitor student progress and identify those 
who may require academic support. This initiative represents a practical step towards data  driven 
academic governance within the Nigerian university environment. 
1.2 Statement of the Problem 
The monitoring of student academic performance is a fundamental obligation of higher education 
institutions. Sustained academic monitoring enables institutions to measure student progress, 
detect signs of academic difficulty early  and implement corrective int erventions before 
performance deteriorates irreversibly. However, in many Nigerian universities, academic 
monitoring systems remain largely manual, fragmented and reactive (Adeyemi & Adeyemi, 2014; 
Nworie, 2012). 
At Kashim Ibrahim University, KIU, as in ma ny comparable institutions, student academic data 
including examination scores, continuous assessment records and attendance figures are collected 
each semester. However, these data are typically analysed in isolation for the purpose of 
calculating semester grades and cumulative grade point averages, rather than being subjected to 
deeper analytical scrutiny. The relationships between multiple performance indicators  and their 
collective influence on academic st  anding are seldom explored in a systematic or a utomated 
manner. 
The volume of student data generated per semester makes manual analysis increasingly 
impractical. A department with several hundred students across multiple levels would require 
significant administrative effort to individually review each student's academic profile and identify 
those who may be struggling. This labour, intensive approach is not scalable, is prone to oversight 
and does not lend itself to the identification of patterns or trends across student cohorts (Han, 
Kamber & Pei, 2012; Witten, Frank & Hall, 2016). 

=== PAGE 5 ===
 
 
Furthermore, the absence of an automated analytical framework means that academic 
administrators lack the tools to cluster or categorise students according to performance 
characteristics. Without such segmentation, it is difficult to design targeted academic interventions 
or to allocate tutorial  and mentorship resources efficiently. Students who fall into high  risk 
performance categories may go unidentified until they have already failed a course or accumulated 
a CGPA below the minimum required st andard at which point the scope for effective intervention 
is considerably diminished. 
Existing literature has demonstrated that machine learning  based systems, particularly those 
employing clustering techniques such as K  means, can effectively segment student populations  
and provide early warning signals for academic underperformance (Dutt, Aghabozrgi & Maizatul, 
2015; Kovacic, 2010; Bydžovská, 2016). However, few such systems have been designed  and 
implemented within Nigerian university setting s and none are documented specifically for Kashim 
Ibrahim University. 
Therefore there is  an identifiable gap between the potential of machine learning analytics to 
support academic monitoring and its current application in many Nigerian universities. This study 
seeks to address that gap by developing a practical, functional  and deployable student 
segmentation and academic monitoring system tailored to the operational realities of a Nigerian 
university environment. 
1.3 Objectives of the Study 
The main objective of this study is to design  and implement a machine learning  based student 
segmentation and academic monitoring system using K  means clustering, with Kashim Ibrahim 
University, KIU, as the case study institution. 
The specific objectives of the study are: 
i. To design  and develop a web  based system that enables authorized administrators to 
upload, manage and process student academic datasets. 
ii. To implement the K  means clustering algorithm to automatically segment students into 
performance groups based on academic indicators including CGPA, attendance rate  and 
assessment scores. 
 

=== PAGE 6 ===
 
 
iii. To provide an academic monitoring interface that enables institutional administrators  and 
academic staff to identify students who may require academic support, interventio n or 
mentorship. 
1.4 Scope of the Study 
This study is concerned with the design  and implementation of a machine learning based student 
segmentation and academic monitoring system. The analytical scope of the study encompasses 
three principal academic perfo rmance indicators: cumulative grade point average (CGPA), 
attendance rate (expressed as a percentage)  and assessment scores (comprising coursework  and 
test marks). These indicators were selected on the basis of their availability within institutional 
records, their recognised influence on overall academic outcomes  and their frequent use in 
comparable educational data mining studies (Romero & Ventura, 2010; Dutt, Aghabozrgi & 
Maizatul, 2015). 
The system developed in this study employs the K  means clustering algorithm for student 
segmentation. The study does not extend to predictive modelling of future academic performance, 
nor does it incorporate natural language processing, deep learning or other advanced machine 
learning paradigms. The clustering analysis is limited to unsupervised grouping of students based 
on the three stated performance variables  and the results are communicated through an 
administrative dashboard. 
Geographically, the study is conducted as a case study of Kashim Ibrahim University, KIU, Borno 
State, Nigeria. The dataset used in the study is sourced from or constructed to reflect the structure 
of student academic records at that institution. The system is designed primarily for use by 
academic administrators  and departmental level staff. It is not intended as a student  facing 
application. 
The study does not address network security architecture, integration with existing university 
enterprise resource planning (ERP) systems  or long term data governance frameworks, as these 
fall outside the defined scope of a final year undergraduate project. These areas are acknowledged 
as important considerations for future development and deployment. 
1.5 Significance of the Study 
This study holds significance at multiple levels institutional, academic and practical.

based on performance data, the system reduces the administrative burden associated with manual

From an academic st  andpoint, this study contributes to the growing body of literature on

international literature on clustering  based academic monitoring systems is well developed 
(Romero & Ventura, 2010; Baker & Yacef, 2009; Pena ,Ayala, 2014), indigenous studies 
contextualized to Nigerian universities remain relatively limited. This work the refore fills an

From a practical st andpoint, the dashboard and reporting features of the system provide decision

More broadly, this study demonstrates that the application of machine learning techniques in 
academic management is both feasible  and beneficial within the resource constraints typical of 
Nigerian universities. It provides a replicable model that other institutions in the country can adapt 
to their local data structures and operational contexts. In doing so, it supports the broader ambition 
of the National Universities Commission to improve the quality  and data responsiveness of  
academic management across the Nigerian higher education system (NUC, 2022).

CHAPTER TWO 
LITERATURE REVIEW 
2.1 Introduction 
This chapter presents a structured review of literature relevant to the design  and implementation 
of a machine learning based student segmentation and academic monitoring system. The review is 
organised to establish a strong conceptual and empirical foundation for the study, beginning with 
the theoretical basis of machine learning  and educational data mining before narrowing 
progressively to clustering methodologies, student performance analysis  and related system 
implementations. The chapter concludes by synthesizing findings from related studies, identifying 
critical gaps in the existing literature and establishing how the present study addresses those gaps 
through a context aware, machine learning driven monitoring framework. 
Literature reviewed in this chapter is drawn from peer  reviewed journal articles, conference 
proceedings, authoritative text  books and technical reports published between 1967  and 2026. 
Selected works include both foundational studies defining the theoretical basis of the techniques 
employed and contemporary studies reflecting current advances in educational data minin g and 
intelligent academic analytics. 
2.2 Machine Learning: Concepts and Theoretical Foundations 
Machine learning is a branch of artificial intelligence concerned with the development of 
computational systems that improve their performance on a defined tas k through experience, 
without being explicitly programmed for each scenario. The widely cited definition by Mitchell 
(1997, p. 2) captures this principle precisely: a computer program learns from experience E with 
respect to a class of tasks T and performance measure P if its performance at T improves with E. 
This formulation distinguishes machine learning from conventional rule  based software, which 
operates strictly on hardcoded logic regardless of data patterns. 
Machine learning is categorised into three principal paradigms. Supervised learning trains a model 
on labelled datasets,where inputs are paired with known outputs ,enabling predictive mappings for 
unseen data. Representative algorithms include decision trees, support vector machines and neural 
networks. Unsupervised learning, by contrast, operates on unlabelled data  and seeks to uncover 
hidden structures through techniques such as clustering  and dimensionality reduction. 
Reinforcement learning involves an agent that learns optimal behaviour throug h environmental 
interaction and reward,based feedback (Bishop, 2006; Russell & Norvig, 2016). The rapid 

=== PAGE 11 ===
 
 
acceleration of machine learning since the early 2000s has been driven by exponential growth in 
data availability, reductions in computational cost  and innovations in algorithm design (LeCun, 
Bengio & Hinton, 2015). 
Within education, machine learning has been applied to student dropout prediction, intelligent 
tutoring, adaptive course recommendation, automated grading  and academic performance 
classification (Kotsiantis, Pierrakeas & Pintelas, 2010; Kemper, Vorhies & Martens, 2020). The 
growing digitalisation of academic administration through student information systems  and 
learning management platforms has further exp anded the availability of structured data that these 
techniques require. 
2.2.1 Supervised vs. Unsupervised Learning in Educational Contexts 
In educational data analysis, the distinction between supervised  and unsupervised learning has 
significant methodological implications. Supervised techniques require datasets pre labelled with 
outcome categories such as 'pass/fail' or 'at  risk/not at risk' ,which presupposes the availability of 
reliable historical classifications. Many institutions, particularly in developing  country contexts, 
lack such consistently labelled records (Bydžovská, 2016). Unsupervised learning eliminates this 
dependency by discovering natural groupings within data based solely on statistical similarity. 
This characteristic makes clustering especially valuable for exploratory ac ademic analytics and 
institutions at early stages of data maturity (Dutt, Aghabozrgi & Maizatul, 2015). The present 
study adopts the unsupervised learning approach precisely because it aligns with the nature of 
available institutional data  and supports pat tern discovery without pre  imposed classification 
assumptions. 
2.3 Educational Data Mining 
Educational Data Mining (EDM) is an interdisciplinary field that applies data mining, machine 
learning and statistical techniques to data generated within educationa l environments, with the 
objective of better underst  anding students and improving learning outcomes (Baker & Yacef, 
2009). The field integrates methodologies from computer science, education  and psychology to 
analyses data sourced from student information  systems, learning management platforms  and 
institutional databases. 
Romero and Ventura (2010), in a l  andmark review of over 300 studies, categorised EDM 
applications into four domains: student modelling, domain knowledge modelling, pedagogical 
support and institutional improvement.  The fourth domain institutional improvement through data 

=== PAGE 12 ===
 
 
driven administrative decision making is most directly relevant to the present study. Pena  Ayala 
(2014) further surveyed 240 EDM studies and found classification to be the most commonly used 
technique, followed by clustering and regression. Crucially, the survey also documented growing 
interest in clustering based approaches for contexts where labelled data is unavailable, reinforcing 
the analytical direction of this research. 
2.3.1 Data Sources and Feature Selection 
EDM systems draw on diverse data types: demographic features (age, gender, geographic 
origin), academic features (grades, CGPA, attendance, assessment scores), engagement features 
(LMS login frequency, assignment submission timing) and socioeconomic attributes. Academic 
features consistently emerge as the most influential predictors of student outcomes across the 
literature (Huang & Fang, 2013; Marburger, 2001). Attendance is of particular significance: 
empirical studies have established a strong positive correlation between consistent class 
attendance and academic achievement and conversely, a pattern of regular absence frequently 
precedes academic decline even among students with comparable prior records (Gatherer & 
Manning, 1998; Marburger, 2001). In this study, three core features, CGPA, attendance rate and 
assessment score, are selected on the basis of theoretical validity, empirical precedent and 
practical availability within the Nigerian institutional context. Because CGPA is only meaningful 
for students with at least one completed semester, this study applies CGPA alongside attendance 
rate and assessment score for returning students, while relying on attendance rate and assessment 
score alone for newly admitted students, who have not yet accumulated a cumulative grade point 
average.

2.5 The K means Clustering Algorithm 
K means is one of the most widely studied and deployed algorithms in data mining, with over five 
decades of application across diverse domains since its original formulation by MacQueen (1967) 
and algorithmic refinement by Lloyd (1982). The algorithm partitions a dataset into K clusters by 
minimizing the Within Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS) ,a measure of intra cluster compactness 
defined as: 
J = Σ(k=1 to K) Σ(xᵢ ∈ Cₖ) ||xᵢ − μₖ||² 
where μₖ is the centroid (mean vector) of clu ster Cₖ and ||xᵢ − μ ₖ||² is the squared Euclidean 
distance. The algorithm operates through four iterative steps: (1) Initialize K centroids; (2) Assign 
each data point to its nearest centroid; (3) Re  compute centroids as the mean of assigned points; 
(4) Repeat until cluster assignments converge. Figure 2.1 illustrates this process conceptually.

2.5.1 K means++ Initialization 
Standard r andom centroid initialization is prone to suboptimal placement, leading to slow 
convergence o r poor final clusters. Arthur  and Vassil vitskii (2007) addressed this with K 
means++, which selects centroids probabilistically in proportion to their squared distance from 
already selected centroids. This ensures wider initial spacing across the feature space, consistently 
improving both cluster quality and convergence speed. K means++ is now the default initialization 
strategy in scikit, learn and most modern ML libraries (Pedregosa et al., 2011). 
2.5.2 Determining the Optimal Number of Clusters 
Three established techniques are applied in this study to determine the appropriate value of K. The 
Elbow Method plots WCSS against increasing K values; the optimal K appears at the 'elbow' 
,where the marginal reduction in WCSS begins to diminish significantly (Ketchen & Shook, 1996). 
The Silhouette Method measures, for each data point, how similar it is to its own cluster relative 
to the nearest alternative; an average silhouette score approaching 1 indicates well  separated 
clusters (Rousseeuw, 1987). The Gap Statistic compares observed WCSS to an expected baseline 
under a reference null distribution, with the optimal K maximising the gap (Tibshirani, Walther & 
Hastie, 2001). Across the EDM literature, K=3 , grouping students into high, average  and at risk 
categories, is the most commonly adopted and institutionally actionable solution (Pal & Pal, 2013; 
Bakar, Rosbi & Hamid, 2015). 
2.5.3 Strengths and Limitations 
K means offers computational efficiency with time complexity of O(nKd) per iteration ,highly 
scalable for large student populations ,alongside interpretable centroid  based cluster summaries 
that can be communicated directly to nontechnical academic stakeholders (Jain, 2010). Its 
limitations are equally well documented: sensitivity to outliers, an assumption of spherical equally 
sized clusters and the requirement to pre  specify K. In the present study, these limitations are

mitigated through feature normalization,  IQR based outlier detection during preprocessing  and 
systematic Elbow and Silhouette analysis for K selection. 
2.6 Student Academic Performance Monitoring in Higher Education 
Academic performance monitoring has evolved from simple record  keeping into a data driven 
institutional function that integrates analytics, predictive modelling  and decision support. York, 
Gibson and Rankin (2015) identify four essential components of effective monitoring systems: 
timely data collection, systematic analysis, targeted intervention  and institutional feedback, 
emphasizing that monitoring's value lies not in data accum ulation alone but in transforming data 
into actionable insights. A critical development in this space is the early alert system, designed to 
identify students at risk before academic failure occurs. Stinebrickner  and Stinebrickner (2014) 
demonstrated that early identification combined with targeted support significantly improves 
student retention, reducing dropout rates by up to 18% among at risk cohorts. 
Despite these advances, adoption of data driven  monitoring remains uneven globally. In the 
Nigerian con text, Adeyemi  and Adeyemi (2014) documented that most universities lack the 
coordinated digital infrastructure necessary for systematic performance monitoring, with 
fragmented data practices, limited technical capacity  and absent analytical frameworks 
compounding the challenge. While Olaleye (2016) and Eze et al. (2018) note gradual progress in 
the adoption of digital academic systems, the integration of machine learning techniques into 
institutional monitoring remains largely absent. This positions the pre sent study at a critical 
intersection between demonstrated international best practice and an underserved national context. 
2.7 Review of Related Works 
This section critically examines prior studies that have applied machine learning and data mining 
techniques to student performance analysis. Rather than merely summarizing existing approaches, 
the review evaluates each study's methodological contributions  and limitations relative to the 
objectives of the present research. 
Furthermore, Kovacic (2010) applied decision trees and logistic regression to enrolment data from 
the Open Polytechnic of New Zeal and, demonstrating that institutional administrative data could 
support meaningful student risk prediction. The study achieved classification accuracies between  
62% and 68% and established the viability of using routinely collected administrative data, rather 
than bespoke assessments, as the basis for academic risk identification. However, its reliance on

interface limited its institutional utility beyond the analytical phase.

Additionally, Pal and Pal (2013) applied K means clustering (K=3) to student examination scores, 
internal assessment marks and attendance at an Indian technical university. The resulting clusters 
mapped onto coherent academic performance categories, high, medium and low with the high  
performance cluster exhibiting significantly higher attendance rates alongside stronger 
examination results. This study is the closest direct precedent to the present work, validating both 
the feature selection  and the algorithm. Its principal limitation is the absence of a system 
implementation: the study remains an offline analysis without the monitoring dashboard o r 
downloadable reporting capabilities that translate cluster insights into institutional action. 
Besides, Dutt et al. (2015) reviewed 33 clustering based EDM studies and found that K means 
accounted for 45% of applications attributed to its efficiency and interpretability while academic 
performance indicators appeared as the primary feature set in 78% of studies. The survey 
highlighted two important deficiencies: insufficient attention to usability for nontechnical 
stakeholders and a near complete absence of studies in sub  Saharan African contexts. Both gaps 
are directly addressed by the present study. 
Equally Important, Bakar et al. (2015) applied K means to student CGPA and semester GPA data 
at a Malaysian public university, determining K=4 as optimal thro ugh silhouette analysis  and 
introducing the concept of longitudinal cluster tracking. Their findings showed that approximately 
22% of at risk students migrated to higher  performing clusters within three semesters, a finding 
that positions cluster , based monitoring as a tool for measuring intervention effectiveness over 
time, not merely for point in time classification. The study did not, however, implement a real time 
monitoring interface. 
Similarly, Bydžovská (2016) compared K means, decision trees, naive Bayes and artificial neural 
networks on student data from Masaryk University. While neural networks achieved the highest 
predictive accuracy, clustering produced more interpretable  and actionable insights for academic 
administrators. This study crystallizes a key design principle for the present work: in institutional 
contexts where nontechnical stakeholders must act on analytical outputs, interpretability is not a 
compromise, it  is a requirement. The  study does not, however, advance beyond comparative 
analysis to system implementation. 
Likewise, Within the Nigerian context, Afolabi et al. (2019) demonstrated the effectiveness of 
classification trees using CGPA  and attendance as predictors, validating the feature selection of 
the present study but without exploring clustering or implementing a monitoring interface.

Olawale, Alabi and Ayo (2021) developed a threshold  based early warning system at a Nigerian 
polytechnic ,demonstrating clear institutional demand for automated monitoring ,but their rule  
based architecture lacks the adaptive, data driven  pattern discovery that machine learning 
clustering provides. Together, these studies confirm both the relevance of performance monitoring 
in Nigeria and the absence of ML based clustering solutions designed specifically for that context.

2.9 Identified Gaps in the Literature 
 
 First, there is a pronounced geographical gap: the substantial majority of EDM studies have been 
conducted in North American, European and Asian institutions. The structural realities of Nigerian 
higher education ,including fragmented data management practices, limited technical 
infrastructure, large class sizes and constrained access to commercial analytics platforms ,are not 
addressed by systems designed for resource rich institutional environments (Adeyemi & Adeyemi, 
2014; Dutt et al., 2015). 
Second, there is a persistent implementa tion gap. While studies such as Pal  and Pal (2013) and 
Bakar et al. (2015) validate the effectiveness of K means for student segmentation at the analytical 
level, none extend their work to a fully deployable, interactive monitoring system. Bydžovská 
(2016) explicitly identified the need for better visualization tools to support nontechnical decision 
makers, yet no study in the reviewed literature implements a comprehensive administrative 
dashboard that converts cluster outputs into actionable institutional intelligence. 
Third and most specifically, there is a methodological gap in the Nigerian literature. Existing 
Nigerian studies, Afolabi et al. (2019) and Olawale et al. (2021), rely on supervised classification 
or static threshold rules, precluding the adaptive, exploratory pattern discovery that unsupervised 
clustering enables. The present study addresses all three gaps through the development of a 
machine learning based student segmentation system, implementing K means++ clustering within 
a web based administrative dash board and situated specifically within the Nigerian university 
context. 
Fourth, none of the reviewed studies distinguish between returning and newly admitted students 
when selecting clustering features. Pal and Pal (2013), Bakar et al. (2015) and Bydžovská (2016) 
all rely on CGPA or semester GPA as a primary clu stering variable without addressing how such 
systems would treat first semester students who have not yet accumulated any GPA history. This 
is not a minor oversight, since a student segmentation system intended for continuous, semester 
by semester institutional use must account for the reality that a portion of the student population 
will always be newly admitted. The present study addresses this gap directly by implementing two 
distinct clustering models, one for returning students incorporating CGPA, atte ndance rate and 
assessment score and one for newly admitted students relying on attendance rate and assessment 

=== PAGE 19 ===
 
 
score alone, ensuring that no student is excluded or misclassified for lack of an academic history 
that new students cannot yet possess.

3.3.1 Segmentation by Student Category: Returning and New Students 
A closer look at the thre e clustering variables in Table 3.1 reveals a practical problem that only 
becomes apparent once real institutional data is considered. Cumulative GPA, as its name implies, 
is a measure that accumulates across multiple semesters of registered coursework. A student who 
has just been admitted into the university has no such history to draw on. Treating a new student's 
CGPA as zero, or substituting some arbitrary placeholder value, would not simply be a harmless 
gap in the data. It would actively mislead the cl ustering algorithm, since K Means has no way of 
knowing that a zero in this context means “not yet applicable” rather than “extremely poor 
academic performance.” New students would either be forced into the same low performing cluster 
as genuinely struggli ng returning students, or would distort the cluster boundaries for everyone 
else depending on how the placeholder value happened to fall relative to the rest of the distribution. 
For this reason, the system implemented in this study does not apply a single  clustering model 
uniformly across the entire student population. Instead, two separate K Means models are trained, 
each operating on the feature set that is actually available and meaningful for the category of 
student concerned. 
The first model, referred to throughout this dissertation as the Returning Students model, is applied 
to students who have completed at least one prior semester and therefore possess an established 
CGPA. This model uses all three variables described in Table 3.1, namely CGPA, Attendance Rate 
and Assessment Score, consistent with the feature justification already given in that section. 
The second model, referred to as the New Students model, is applied to students in their first 
semester at the institution. Since these students have  no CGPA to contribute, this model is 
restricted to the two variables that are available from day one of enrolment, Attendance Rate and 
Assessment Score. Table 3.1(b) summarizes this distinction.

3.8.1 Model Evaluation Strategy: Train Test Validation 
A recurring question in unsupervised learning is how a clustering model can be evaluated at all, 
since there are no preexisting labels against which predictions can be checked for accuracy in the 
way a supervised classifier would be tested. This does not mean the model escapes evaluation 
altogether. What can and should be tested is whether the clusters a model discovers on one portion 
of the data continue to hold up when the model is confronted with records it has never seen. If the 
structure the algorithm finds is genuine and not simply an artefact of the particular sample it  was 
trained on, then new records should fall into similarly well formed clusters when assigned using 
the same centroids. 
To test this, both the Returning Students model and the New Students model in this study follow 
a train test split, with eighty percen t of each cleaned dataset used for training and the remaining 
twenty percent held back entirely during training. The split is performed after outlier removal but 
before normalization, and the Min Max scaler itself is fitted only on the training portion, si nce 
allowing information from the test set to influence the scaling would quietly leak knowledge of 
unseen data back into the model and overstate how well it generalizes (Witten, Frank and Hall, 
2016). 
K Means is then trained on the eighty percent training  set in the usual way described in Sect ion 
3.8, using K Means plus  initialization with K set to three. The resulting centroids are treated as 
fixed. Each record in the twenty percent test set is then assigned to whichever of these three 
centroids it lies closest to, without any retraining or adjustment of the centroids themselves. Two 
diagnostics are computed for both the training set and the test set separately, the Within Cluster 
Sum of Squares and the average Silhouette Score. If these two figures r emain close between the 
training and test sets, this is taken as evidence that the clustering structure is stable and not merely 
memorized noise specific to the training sample. A sharp deterioration in test set performance, by 
contrast, would indicate that the model had over fit to the training data and could not be trusted to 
classify new students reliably each semester. 
This evaluation is carried out independently for each of the two models, since the Returning 
Students model and the New Students model operate on different feature sets and populations and 
therefore need to be validated on their own terms rather than pooled together.

3.11 Ethical Considerations 
The research involves student academic data, which constitutes personally identifiable information 
requiring appropria te safeguards. Four ethical principles govern the study. First, data 
anonymisation: all real student records used in prototype development are anonymised by 
replacing names with pseudonyms  and matriculation numbers with synthetic identifiers  and no 
real st udent data appears in any figure, table  or report in this dissertation. Second, data

hashing, session based authentication and role based access control, ensuring that database records

administrators that cluster membership must be interpreted a longside contextual information and
